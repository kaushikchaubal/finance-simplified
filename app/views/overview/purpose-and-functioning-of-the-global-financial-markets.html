What are Capital Markets? Markets are places where buyers and sellers of goods or services interact Capital Markets are markets in which buyers and sellers interact with respect to capital (i.e. funds, money) Specifically, Capital Markets are where: Individuals, governments, and businesses with funds transfer those funds ... ... to other individuals, governments and businesses ... in exchange for an economic interest or a promised set of future payments Stock and bond markets are two major capital markets Purpose and Functioning of the Global Markets Helping businesses raise money to facilitate commercial trade through borrowing or raising capital Generating income and long-term value creation for individual and institutional investors Speculation Hedging financial risk Managing the economy Financial Market Activities / Uses Real money activity Borrowing Capital financing requirements for firms Facilitating commercial trade Cashflow management in corporate treasuryoperations Speculative borrowing Lending and investing Institutional portfolio investment Cashflow management in corporate treasury operations Speculative lending Equity investments Institutional portfolio investment Speculative trading Foreign exchange Carry trading Other speculative trading Hedging financial risk Derivative and leveraged activity Speculation Hedging financial risk Arbitrage Transaction flow based activity Banks Brokers Scalpers Management of the economy Central bank policy rates “The Market” Defined The meeting place where willing buyers and sellers negotiate on prices for executing financial transactions. Can be: a physical central marketplace, e.g. New York Stock Exchange floor an electronic central marketplace e.g. London Stock Exchange, Eurex a telephone or computer-based direct dealing marketplace e.g. OTC derivatives a brokered marketplace e.g. inter-dealer brokers, institutional brokers, electronic brokers etc The collective noun for participants (e.g. the market was surprised by) The generality of prices (e.g. the market was stronger today) The abbreviation for the market price (e.g. the market is trading 120.15) Where there is no single venue for trading often there is no single market price Often only indicative prices are visible on information systems such as Bloomberg or Reuters Major Financial Markets Money markets the markets for short-term (<=1yr) debt finance E.g. commercial paper, short-term US Treasuries, certificates of deposit Fixed-income (bond) markets the market for long-term (>1yr) debt finance principal markets in: US, Japan, Germany, Italy, UK Equities (stock) market the markets for shares in companies equity finance principal markets in: US, Japan, UK, France, Germany, Canada, Switzerland Foreign-exchange and commodities markets the market something else (e.g. cash market instruments, indexes) trading in derivatives exceeds cash market volume in many markets Capital Markets can be taken to mean all of the above sometimes restricted to money, bond, and equities markets sometimes restricted just to long-term finance markets, i.e. bond and equities markets Major Financial Centers - Primary: LONDON, NEW YORK, TOKYO Secondary: Negotiable Securities Markets Negotiable securities derive their value from The performance of the issuer The terms of the instrument Negotiable securities are designed to be easily sold and resold Also known as “financial paper” as were originally documents (or receipts) In the case of debt securities holding title indicates creditor-ship to the issuer In the case of equities holding title indicates ownership of the issuer Modern paperless market simplifies trading Electronic registration and custody Payment versus deliver systems reduce settlement risk Trades in these markets are sometimes referred to as “bargains” Seller delivers the securities in exchange for a financial consideration (i.e. the payment) Buyer makes payment in exchange for the securities Counterparties agree on consideration (i.e. price) but disagree on valuation (i.e. cheapness) Traded Contract Markets Traded contracts derive their value from The performance of the trade counterparties The negotiated terms of the contracts themselves The negotiated terms are usually governed by market conventions or standards This simplifies dealing by reducing errors Some typical negotiable terms within a traded contract are: Trade price: The number which forms the basis of payments made under the contract Effective date: The date on which a contractual obligation to perform becomes effective Settlement dates: The dates on which payments are to be made Maturity date: The date on which a contractual obligation has been fully met So called “derivatives” can involve contingent delivery of an underlying instrument (or asset) or involve payments based on its value The price of derivatives therefore derives from the value of the underlying Derivatives can also be packaged as securities Derivatives markets can be contrasted with so called “cash” markets Deals in traded contracts are sometimes referred to in terms of buying or selling even though no immediate transfer of a security or other asset is involved Bespoke markets for contracts (e.g. exotic options) Trading activity is episodic and driven by requests to deal Even so market standards and conventions exist Resemble primary markets in securities Marketplaces for contracts (e.g. the inter-dealer deposit market) Trading activity is continuous during trading hours and driven by price competition Limited choice of effective and maturity dates quoted E.g. O/N, 1W, 2W, 1M, 2M, 3M, 6M, 12M Other dates available on demand Negotiation only occurs on price and quantity Resembles secondary markets in securities but are in fact primary markets (in most cases) Price driven inter-dealer markets can evolve from deal driven markets over time Derivatives exchanges Auction markets with centralized order flow Can only list futures and simple options due to constraints in margining system Very efficient and deeply liquid markets act as benchmarks for inter-dealer market Examples: CME, Eurex, Euronext.liffe Products and Players (** Diagram here**)
